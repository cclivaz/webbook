Part I. Introduction

The research question of HumaReC is to inquiry, from the test-case of the edition of an unique trilingual Greek, Latin and Arabic New Testament manuscript, how Humanities research is reshaped and transformed by the digital rhythm of data production and publication. The challenge of the project is to develop a new model of Humanities research that affords a continuous publication of data and analysis as well as the evaluation of experts, working in collaboration with librarians, an editorial board of experts and a scientific publisher, Brill. The edition and study of the Proto-Pauline letters in the trilingual New Testament manuscript, Marciana Gr. Z. 11 (379), will be the test case for the development of these new practices.

The digital opening in Humanities is transforming the scheme and rhythm of research beyond printed material and is creating a completely new research paradigm. Publishing formats can now be short syntheses in blogs, draft versions of papers, social media posts, videos – even before the research is completed and peer-reviewed. Furthermore, many projects are conducted online from beginning to end. Academic institutions, Universities or Libraries, support more and more this kind of digital projects. Next to the gain of time between research and publication, new publishing formats offer many other advantages.

They can integrate other medias than writing (images, sounds, videos) and can link to other resources.

The flexibility of the digital support allows to pay attention to the reactions of readers/users and to adapt the research directions or results if required.

Openness is also a key concept that digital research projects can provide more easily compared to traditional writing formats.1

In addition, the online presence is a great opportunity to give more visibility to objects that were neglected by the research as it is the case for the Arabic New Testament in general and for the Marciana Gr. Z. 11 (379) in particular. In fact this trilingual manuscript was never studied in details. In the project HumaReC, a special focus will be given to the Pauline letters, as continuation of our previous research.

Next to these positive aspects, the present situation raises some concerns and challenges. The question of the expertise and certification is one of them. New ways of publishing contribute to the blurring of frontiers between un-peer-reviewed and peer-reviewed writings, since these formerly separated steps are increasingly overlapping. Moreover, any digital projects, notably digital editions, simply do not include the step of external reviewing.

Having this in mind, the project HumaReC aims to develop a new model of Humanities research by taking advantage of the new publication rhythm and by establishing better practices.

This first part presents the main principles of the project; we first relate the genesis of the project, which is the continuation of a first project on the Arabic manuscript of Paul. Secondly, we present how the model proposed by HumaReC try to respond to issues and challenges induced by the digital turn in the Humanities.

The part II is dedicated to the online platform of HumaReC and its various aspects. The part III is dedicated to the study of the manuscript at the core of this project, Marciana Gr. Z. 11 (379).

Besides of the ideal that open access represents, it becomes also more and more mandatory. This is now e.g. a requirement of the Swiss National Science Foundation: “The results of research financed by public funds should be published electronically so that they are freely and immediately available without charge and can thus be reused by third parties, since they are considered as a public good.”, 'Open Access to Publications'.
