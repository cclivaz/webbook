7.1 Codicological and paleographical features

General

Marciana Gr. Z. 11 (379) is a trilingual manuscript containing Acts, Catholic letters and Pauline letters in three columns in Greek, Latin and Arabic. This aspect is particularly interesting: in fact, as we said before, it is the only New Testament trilingual manuscript containing Greek, Latin and Arabic that is currently known by the research (GA 460).

The codex has 304 folios of ca. 28,7x24 cm and each column is ca. 21x5 cm big.

The codex was written on parchment.1 Thus, we find features specific to parchment as follicle patterns, holes due to streching of the skin or other irregularities.

A few folios are missing at the beginning and the end of the codex. The book of Acts beginns with Ac 1:12 and Philemon is missing. The initial argumentum attests to its earlier presence. The foliotation in Arabic number (on the recto in the right corner) is more recent: the first folio bears the number 1.

Mioni give in the catalogue the details of the quires:

I septem ff. (f. 7), 8 quaterniones (f. 71), I ternio (olim quaternio, f. 77), 16 quat. (f. 205), I septem ff. (f. 212), 8 quaterniones (f. 276), I septem ff. (f. 283), I quinque ff. (f. 288), 2 quat. (f. 304). [Mioni 1985: 16].

Similar manuscripts

 The manuscript is very similar to two other manuscripts at least:

  • BL Harl. 5786 (year 1153), a trilingual Greek-Latin-Arabic Psalter. The images of the manuscript are online on the British Library website. It was copied before 1153 and is considered as of Sicilian origin.
  • Marciana Gr. Z. 539 (303) (12th), a manuscript containing the Gospels in Greek and Arabic. It contains the four Gospels in Greek and Arabic (GA 211 in the Gregory-Aland list). This manuscript aroused the interest of researchers working on a specific family of Greek New Testament manuscripts, family 13. [Lafleur 2012: 128] The microfilm of the manuscript are available in the NT Virtual Manuscript Room but this requires an inscription.

The context of production of the three manuscripts Marciana Gr. Z. 11 (379), Psalter BL Harl. 5786 and Marciana Gr. Z. 539 (303) seems to have been Sicily (see e.g. [Mioni 1985: 16]; [Nef 2008]). We investigate this question in the next chapter.

The three manuscripts share similarities regarding the size of the codex, the layout of the pages and the writings. We come back to this point after the paleographical remarks below.

In addition to BL Harl. 5786 and Marciana Gr. Z. 539 (303), we have to mention here two other manuscripts: BNF supplément grec 911 and Naples Biblioteca Nazionale Gr. 20. These manuscripts are – with the three manuscripts mentioned above – described by Paul Géhin and later by Annliese Nef (Nef 2008) as witnesses of the existence of Arabicized Christians in Sicily. However, these two manuscripts are not that close to Marciana Gr. Z. 11 (379), BL Harl. 5786 and Marciana Gr. Z. 539 (303).

  • BNF supplément grec 911 is in fact more archaic: its colophon mentions the year 1041. The southern Italian origin is shown according to Géhin by the features of the Greek (script "as de pique") and the Arabic scripts [Géhin 1997]. Other scholars have questioned the Western origin of the whole manuscript:
    [...] the Arabic writing is definitey Easter. Therefore, where was the MS written? [...] we can also conjecture that after the Greek copy was made in the South of Italy the MS was sent to Eastern land (to some oriental monastery in Palestine) where the Arabic translation was made according to the ready Greek text. [Urbán Fernández 2007]
    Afterwards, the manuscript was still in use in Palestine until the 19th century. [Monferrer-Sala 2013]
  • Naples Biblioteca Nazionale Gr. 20 is a Greek Psalter (11th century?) with an Latin and an Arabic translations in the margin. We were not able to see pictures of this manuscript but it does not seem to correspond to the well-made layout of the bilingual or trilingual manuscripts mentioned above. However, this does not exclude a similar production milieu.
